4/14/25, 4:05â€¯PM - SANJAY Sk: 1. Ethics washing in AI governance
Ethics washing refers to the practice of using ethical language or frameworks to appear responsible without making meaningful changes. It allows organizations to avoid regulatory oversight by showcasing superficial ethics initiatives. This misleads stakeholders and undermines genuine ethical efforts in AI development.

2. Need for ethics in AI
Ethics in AI ensures responsible development and use of AI technologies. It addresses issues like bias, transparency, accountability, and privacy. Ethical AI helps protect human rights, promotes fairness, and builds public trust in AI systems.

3. Two common AI ethics principles

Fairness: Ensuring AI systems do not discriminate and treat all individuals equally.

Transparency: Making AI decisions understandable and explainable to users and regulators.


4. Value-Sensitive Design (VSD)
VSD is an approach to designing technology that accounts for human values throughout the design process. In AI, it involves integrating ethical principles like privacy, autonomy, and equity into system architecture and functionality from the beginning.

5. Human rights at the core of AI ethics
AI can affect fundamental rights such as privacy, freedom, and non-discrimination. Placing human rights at the core ensures AI systems support dignity, equity, and justice, minimizing harms and promoting social good.

6. Two challenges of self-regulation in AI

Lack of enforcement: Voluntary codes may not be followed strictly.

Conflict of interest: Companies may prioritize profit over ethical concerns without external accountability.


7. Safety case in regulatory governance
A safety case is a structured argument supported by evidence that a system is acceptably safe for a specific application. It is widely used in safety-critical domains like healthcare and transportation and is now being applied to AI systems to demonstrate safety compliance.

8. High elastic algorithm
An algorithm is considered high elastic when it can adapt to a wide range of data inputs and contexts without significant loss of performance. These algorithms are flexible but may also introduce unpredictability or increased risks in decision-making.

9. Stakeholder consultation in high-risk AI
Involving stakeholders helps identify potential risks and unintended consequences early. It ensures diverse perspectives are considered, increasing the system's acceptability, fairness, and robustness in high-stakes environments.

10. Potential harms caused by AI
AI can lead to discrimination, privacy invasion, job displacement, misinformation, and autonomous decision-making failures. These harms may affect individuals, groups, or societies if not addressed through ethical and technical safeguards.

11. AI vigilance
AI vigilance involves continuous monitoring and assessment of AI systems to ensure they operate as intended. It includes updating algorithms, detecting biases, and responding to unforeseen consequences, ensuring long-term ethical and safe functioning.

12. Human rights-centered oversight
This approach ensures that AI systems respect, protect, and promote human rights. Oversight mechanisms should evaluate whether AI aligns with principles like fairness, dignity, privacy, and freedom from discrimination.

13. Professional ethics & multidisciplinary need
Professional ethics refers to the moral responsibilities of AI practitioners. AI development requires a multidisciplinary approach because it impacts legal, social, psychological, and ethical domains. This diversity ensures well-rounded, human-centered solutions.

14. Accountability in computer systems
Accountability means that system designers, developers, and operators are answerable for their systemsâ€™ outcomes. It includes traceability of decisions, transparency, and the ability to identify who is responsible in case of failures or harms.

15. Oversight vs. Accountability
Oversight refers to ongoing supervision and regulation, while accountability involves responsibility for outcomes. Effective AI governance needs both: oversight to guide actions and accountability to ensure consequences for harmful decisions.

16. Unit of analysis in AI accountability
The unit of analysis is the entity (e.g., algorithm, model, organization) being evaluated for accountability. It helps identify who or what should be held responsible and ensures clarity in assessing ethical and legal responsibilities.

17. Goodhartâ€™s Law & algorithmic transparency
Goodhartâ€™s Law states that "when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure." In AI, over-optimizing for specific metrics can lead to manipulation or unintended behaviors, highlighting the need for transparent and holistic evaluation metrics.
